## Questions

1. Git tracks the *history* of your project, not just the current state. Explain in your own words what a "commit" really is — what information does it store, and why does having a chain of commits give you more power than just saving a file? Use an analogy from real life (not from programming) to support your answer.

2. Compare the **working directory**, the **staging area**, and the **repository** in Git. Why does Git have a staging area at all — why not just commit everything at once? Describe a real scenario where the staging area saves a developer from making a mistake.

3. The `.gitignore` file is one of the most important files in any project. Explain why certain files (like `.env`, `venv/`, `__pycache__/`) should never be committed to Git. What real-world consequences can happen if a developer forgets to add `.env` to `.gitignore` and pushes to a public GitHub repository?

4. A developer on your team writes commit messages like: `"fix"`, `"update stuff"`, `"working now"`, `"final"`, `"final2"`. You have to review their code six months later. Describe the problems this causes, and explain what a good commit message strategy looks like and why it matters for a team.
